01 — Introduction

Why are our oceans in danger?

The oceans cover more than 70% of Earth's surface and are the blue lungs of our planet: they produce over 50% of the oxygen we breathe, regulate the climate, and host an immense diversity of life. Yet decades of uncontrolled human activity have pushed them into an unprecedented crisis.

Marine pollution comes from many sources: single-use plastics, oil spills, untreated sewage, agricultural fertilizers, and industrial waste. All of these agents destroy habitats, poison species, and disrupt the ocean's natural cycles.

⚠️ Every year approximately 8 million tons of plastic enter the ocean — equivalent to dumping a garbage truck into the sea every single minute.

02 — Causes

Main sources of pollution

🧴 Plastic Pollution

Plastic is the most visible pollutant in our oceans. Bags, bottles, microplastics, and packaging waste drift in massive garbage patches — the most notorious being the Great Pacific Garbage Patch, estimated to be larger than France. Marine animals mistake plastic for food, leading to blockages and death.

🛢️ Oil Spills

Accidents involving tanker ships and oil platforms release millions of liters of crude oil that form slicks on the water's surface, blocking sunlight and oxygen exchange. Seabirds, turtles, and marine mammals become trapped in the oil and die from suffocation or poisoning.

🌾 Agricultural & Industrial Runoff

Fertilizers washed from farmland carry excess nitrogen and phosphorus into the sea, triggering massive algal blooms (red tides) that consume dissolved oxygen and create dead zones where no life can survive. Factories, meanwhile, discharge heavy metals such as mercury, lead, and cadmium.

🌡️ Acidification & Climate Change

CO₂ released by burning fossil fuels is absorbed by the ocean, lowering its pH in a process called ocean acidification. This dissolves coral reefs and mollusc shells, destroying entire ecosystems that support marine life — and ultimately, human food chains.

03 — Consequences

Effects on ecosystems and people

Ocean degradation does not only affect marine creatures — its consequences ripple through the entire food chain, reaching humans as well:

🐠 Loss of Biodiversity

Hundreds of species of fish, turtles, dolphins, and whales are threatened with extinction due to direct pollution and habitat destruction. Coral reefs — often called the "tropical rainforests of the sea" — have lost more than 50% of their coverage in the last 30 years.

🧬 Microplastics in the Food Chain

Plastics break down into microscopic particles called microplastics, which are ingested by plankton and fish. Recent studies have detected microplastics in human blood, breast milk, and even at the summit of Mount Everest. We consume plastic without even knowing it.

🌊 Climate Disruption

Healthy oceans absorb heat and CO₂ from the atmosphere. A degraded ocean loses that capacity, accelerating climate change: more hurricanes, severe droughts, melting glaciers, and coastal communities permanently at risk of flooding.

04 — Solutions

What can we do?

The situation is serious, but not irreversible. There are actions we can all take — as individuals, communities, and governments — that can make a real difference:

♻️
Reduce single-use plastics and recycle properly
🐟
Choose seafood from sustainable sources
🏖️
Join beach and river clean-up events
📢
Demand stricter laws against industrial pollution

Organizations such as Ocean Conservancy, Greenpeace, and the UN Ocean Programme work with governments and communities to restore marine ecosystems, establish marine protected areas, and cut the emissions driving acidification.
